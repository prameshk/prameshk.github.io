Welcome to Computer Science Theory

Mathematics teaches us to ask questions like `What is the value of f(x)?', `Do any objects of type Y exist?', and `Is Z true?' Theoretical computer science builds on this tradition, expanding our field of inquiry to include questions like `How much time (and other limited resources) does it take to evaluate f(x)?', `Can I write down formal instructions for finding a Y?', and `Can the truth of Z be computed?' In theoretical computer science, we define problems formally and think about how hard they are to solve under various models of computation.

Are you curious about the fundamental nature of computation? What makes some tasks `hard', and others `easy'? The mathematical tools theorists use to formally define concepts of security, privacy, and equity? This course may be for you. (Not convinced? Try Sipser's friendly argument in the preface of the textbook.)

The course is highly abstract: there will be no programming and problem sets will revolve around formal proofs. Students are expected to be comfortable with discrete mathematics and proofs. (For a refresher, see Chapter 0 of the textbook or the Lecture 0 recording, coming soon.)

Lectures

Lecture 0 [Video]

Optional lecture: a quick review of discrete mathematics material.
Reading: Preface: `To the Student' and `To the Educator.' Review Chapter 0 (pp. 1-25).

Lecture 1 (6/28)

Introduction to class. Alphabets, strings, and languages. Discrete finite automata and regular langauges.

Lecture 2 (6/30)

Finite languages are regular. Non-deterministic finite automata (NFAs). Languages are regular if and only if recognized by an NFA.


No class on 7/5 (University holiday.)

Lecture 3 (7/7)

Converting an NFA to a DFA is very expensive. Regular languages are closed under

complement, union, intersection, concatenation, power, and Kleene star.

Lecture 4 (7/12)

Regular expressions and the pumping lemma.

Lecture 5 (7/14)

Proving languages irregular using closure properties. Equivalence relations. Context-free grammars and languages.

Lecture 6 (7/19)

The language of valid regular expressions is context-free but not regular. The class of CFLs is closed under union, concatenation, etc. Every regular language is context-free.

Lecture 7 (7/21)

Languages that aren't context free. CFLs not closed under instersection or complement. Pushdown automata. Turing Machines.

Lecture 8 (7/26)

Deciding and recognizing a language. Hierarchy of language classes we've seen thus far. Nondeterministic Turing Machines.

Lecture 9 (7/28)

Decidable languages are closed under complement. Showed several languages are decidable. Defined cardinality and countability of sets.

Lecture 10 (8/2)

The set of real numbers is not countable. A_TM is not decidable and its complement isn't recognizable. Recognizable languages not closed under complement. Turing reductions.

Lecture 11 (8/4)

Rice's theorem. Mapping reducibility.
